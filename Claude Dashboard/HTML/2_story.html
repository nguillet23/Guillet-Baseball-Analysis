Can Statcast data predict what a batter will do before the season ends?

Models were trained exclusively on 2019–2024 data — exit velocity, barrel rate, sprint speed, swing metrics — then applied to 2025 batters who were never seen during training. This is the story of what worked, what didn't, and who beat the machine.

Chapter 1 — The setup

Training on history,
predicting the future

The central question: can a model trained on six seasons of Statcast data predict a player's counting stats in a year it has never seen? We used four model types — Linear, Ridge, Lasso, and Random Forest — for each target stat, then selected the best performer by held-out R² before freezing the model for 2025 deployment.

The Measurable Batting (MB) formula combines those component stats:
MB = ((H + BB − CS) × (TB + 0.7 × SB)) / (AB + BB + CS)
xMB substitutes each component with its model-predicted equivalent wherever the model's training R² clears a 0.50 threshold.
Chapter 2 — Model performance

Which stats could the model actually predict?

The bars below show out-of-sample R² for each predicted component in 2025. AB and H were the most predictable — player playing time and contact profiles are relatively stable year-over-year. SB and CS were harder; stolen base decisions are more situational and manager-dependent than raw contact quality.

Chapter 3 — xMB construction

Which components earned a spot in xMB?

Each component is evaluated against the R² threshold of 0.50. Pass: the expected value (x<stat>) is used. Fail: the actual observed stat is used as a fallback. This hybrid approach prevents a weak model from corrupting the final xMB prediction.
